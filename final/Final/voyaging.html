Polynesian Voyaging: Ancestors of the Pacific

Native Hawaiians descend from master navigators who voyaged thousands of miles across open ocean using only stars, waves, and birds.

Wayfinding Without Instruments

Polynesian voyagers from the Marquesas and Society Islands reached Hawaiʻi between 300-1200 AD using non-instrument navigation called wayfinding. They read ocean swells, wind patterns, cloud formations, and star paths to find tiny islands in vast Pacific.

Navigators memorized hundreds of stars and their rising/setting positions. Hōkūleʻa (star of gladness) guided voyages to southern destinations while other stars marked northern paths back to Hawaiʻi.

Hōkūleʻa: Modern Revival

In 1976, the Polynesian Voyaging Society built Hōkūleʻa, the first voyaging canoe in 600 years. Its successful voyage from Hawaiʻi to Tahiti proved ancient wayfinding still worked, sparking the Hawaiian Renaissance.

Today Hōkūleʻa continues worldwide voyages promoting mālama honua (care for our island Earth) and connecting Pacific cultures.
