Historical Overview

Hawaiʻi was settled by Polynesian voyagers who arrived by voyaging canoe and developed a distinct society, including aliʻi (chiefs), kapu (sacred laws), and highly organized systems of land and resource management.

Later, contact with Western powers, the rise of the sugar economy, and political pressure led to the overthrow of the Hawaiian Kingdom and eventual annexation to the United States.

Key Dates in Hawaiian History

Selected events that shaped Hawaiian culture and sovereignty
Year Event Significance
c. 400–800 CE Early Polynesian settlement Voyagers from other Pacific islands establish communities and lay the foundations of Hawaiian culture.
1810 Unification under Kamehameha I The Hawaiian Islands are united as a single kingdom, centralizing political power.
1893 Overthrow of the Hawaiian Kingdom A group of businessmen and political leaders deposes Queen Liliʻuokalani, ending the monarchy.
1898 Annexation by the United States Hawaiʻi becomes a U.S. territory, accelerating cultural and political changes.
1959 Statehood Hawaiʻi becomes the 50th state, while Native Hawaiians continue efforts to protect language, culture, and land.
