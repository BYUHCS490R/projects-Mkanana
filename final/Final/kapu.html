The Kapu System: Sacred Laws

Before 1819, kapu governed all aspects of Hawaiian life, connecting aliʻi (chiefs), kahuna (priests), and makaʻāinana (commoners) through spiritual and social order.

What Was Kapu?

Kapu means "forbidden" or "set apart." It created harmony between people, gods, and nature. Examples: women couldn't eat pork or bananas, commoners couldn't let shadows touch aliʻi, fishing had seasonal restrictions.

Violating kapu brought severe punishment, often death, because it disrupted spiritual balance (mana). Heiau temples enforced religious kapu through rituals and offerings.

End of Kapu (Ai Noa)

In 1819, Kamehameha II (Liholiho) broke kapu by eating with women at a feast called ʻAi Noa (free eating). This ended the traditional religion and opened doors for Christianity.

The shift allowed new social freedoms but also rapid Western influence on Hawaiian society and governance.
